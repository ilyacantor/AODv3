Here is my breakdown of the situation and a strategic recommendation on how to handle these specific cases.1. The Verdict: Solid Root Cause AnalysisYour assessment is spot on. The system is functioning "as designed" based on standard Alias Collapsing rules. The friction comes purely from Policy Alignment.AOD is prioritizing a clean, grouped view (Parent Entity).Farm is prioritizing a granular, enumerative view (Individual Assets).2. The Nuance: Not All Aliases Are EqualWhile the root cause is the same for all 8, the solution should arguably differ based on the nature of the asset. We are looking at two distinct categories here:Category A: Infrastructure & TLD Variants (Zoom & Adobe)Assets: zoom.us, zoomapp.io, zoom-meetings.net, adobelogin.comAnalysis: These are functional redirects or infrastructure supports for the main parent domain. zoom.us is Zoom. adobelogin.com is just an auth portal for Adobe.Risk Profile: Shared infrastructure, shared policy, shared SOC.Recommendation: Collapse. These should remain rolled up to zoom.com and adobe.com. The "Farm" expectations should be updated to accept the parent domain as a pass for these.Category B: Distinct Product Lines (Atlassian Family)Assets: trello.com, bitbucket.orgAnalysis: These are acquisitions that often retain distinct infrastructure, separate codebases, and different tech stacks (and distinct vulnerabilities) for years after acquisition.Risk Profile: trello.com has a different attack surface than atlassian.net. Collapsing them obscures the specific risks associated with Trello.Recommendation: Separate (Standalone). From a security/ASM perspective, Trello and Bitbucket are significant enough to stand alone.Category C: The "Zombie" Asset (HipChat)Asset: hipchat.comAnalysis: As you noted, this is a discontinued product.Recommendation: Collapse. Keeping it under Atlassian is appropriate for "zombie detection" (monitoring for subdomain takeovers on legacy DNS entries) without cluttering the active asset list.3.
